## I. Thesis & Market

### Q1. Why is settlement the right slot to anchor, vs. agent issuance or coordination?
Three reasons. First, settlement is the layer with the strongest network-effect dynamics — once an agent economy standardizes on a settlement substrate, switching costs compound. Second, settlement is the slot where almost no credible team is building; Virtuals/Olas/Fetch are all anchored above it. Third, settlement is upstream of every other agent activity — issuance, coordination, deployment all eventually clear through a settlement layer. Anchoring there is structurally aligned with where category economics will concentrate.

### Q2. How big is the addressable market, conservatively?
Public 2026 research from Messari, a16z, and Delphi converges on an AI Agent infrastructure TAM growing from approximately $8B to over $50B within five years. Settlement infrastructure for agent economies is a subset of that, but a structurally significant one — historical analogues (Visa, Stripe, ACH) capture single-digit percentages of total transaction value, which at $50B+ TAM compounds quickly.

### Q3. What's the moat?
Three layers, in order of durability:
1. **First-mover positioning** at the settlement layer specifically — the slot most agent-economy projects haven't yet attempted to claim
2. **Production infrastructure already running** since 2021 — not a roadmap, an operating stack
3. **EMURGO backing + Cardano-native heritage** — structural credibility plus an ecosystem-native distribution channel

## II. Token Economics

### Q4. Why is AA capped at 100M?
Fixed supply enables clear long-term economic modeling for agents that hold AA as collateral or unit of account. Inflation tolerance is meaningfully different for non-human counterparties than for human ones; agents discount forward-supply expansion deterministically. Fixed-supply design is structurally aligned with the use case.

## III. Execution & Risk

### Q8. The current TVL is small. Why does that not concern you?
It should concern any investor who values traction over positioning. Our thesis is that we are intentionally building rails ahead of the traffic — TVL is the wrong measure of progress at this stage. The right measure is whether the execution layer ships in production beta within two quarters, whether at least one external team deploys agents on it, and whether the multi-chain expansion lands. Those are the metrics worth tracking. If we miss those, the thesis breaks.

### Q9. What's the dependency risk on UXLINK?
The UXLINK alliance is a distribution accelerator, not a dependency. Even without UXLINK, the execution layer is independently deployable. UXLINK's role is to compress time-to-distribution; their failure or pivot does not invalidate the underlying thesis.

### Q10. What happens if the AI agent economy doesn't materialize at the projected scale?
Two failure modes worth considering. First, if the agent economy materializes but at a smaller scale, the four-product stack remains a functioning DeFi infrastructure — we revert to the original addressable market with no structural damage. Second, if the entire agent narrative collapses, we are in the same position as every other infrastructure project that bet on category emergence. The asymmetric upside is significant; the downside is bounded by the existing DeFi stack remaining operational.

### Q12. Competitive risk from L1 / L2 chains building native agent settlement?
This is the largest structural risk and one worth taking seriously. Ethereum, Solana, and the major L2s could in principle build native agent settlement primitives. Our positioning relies on three asymmetries: (a) we're already running production infrastructure those chains have not built; (b) settlement is application-layer, not consensus-layer, so the L1s are not the natural builders; (c) being chain-agnostic (multi-chain roadmap) lets us serve agents wherever they land, rather than betting on one substrate winning.
